According to Schopenhauer, the relationship between will, suffering, and art can be understood as follows:

The will is the fundamental force that drives existence, and it is responsible for the suffering that pervades life (Passage 4). However, this suffering can be alleviated through the contemplation of beauty in art. Art allows us to transcend the will's influence and behold the objective, pure nature of things, unencumbered by our own desires and interests (Passage 1).

In fact, Schopenhauer argues that the pleasure we derive from beauty in art is a result of momentarily escaping the constant sorrow and suffering caused by the will. Art enables us to contemplate the "purely knowable" side of the world, which is free from pain (Passage 3). This appreciation of beauty can even bring solace to those who are suffering, as it provides a distraction from their affliction.

Moreover, Schopenhauer suggests that art can be seen as a means of coping with suffering. The artist, who is driven by the will's influence, can temporarily forget about their own suffering through the act of creation (Passage 3). This creative process becomes an end in itself for the artist, providing momentary relief from the constant pain.

However, it is essential to note that this artistic escapism does not eliminate suffering entirely. As Schopenhauer notes, even the artist who finds solace in art still remains chained to the will's influence and must ultimately return to their own suffering (Passage 2). Nevertheless, art can provide a momentary respite from the constant sorrow caused by the will.

In summary, Schopenhauer sees the relationship between will, suffering, and art as follows: the will drives existence and causes suffering, but art provides a means of temporarily escaping this suffering through the contemplation of beauty. This appreciation of beauty can bring solace to those who are afflicted, and even serve as a coping mechanism for artists struggling with their own pain.
